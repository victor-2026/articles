I prepped using the official Meta guide. Then I talked to people who actually sat the interview.

5 things the guide didn't mention:

**1. The AI in the real interview is deliberately weaker**
Practice environments give you a helpful AI. The real Meta interview gives you a "nerfed" one — it describes functionality instead of pointing out bugs. Practice = training wheels. Real interview = no training wheels.

**2. Recruiters read your entire prompt history**
After the interview, recruiters review every prompt you sent. Disorganized, exploratory prompts count against you. Every prompt should show intent — not "let me try this."

**3. Pipeline your workflow**
While AI drafts a function, use that time to explain the next step to the interviewer. This is called pipelining. It shows you're the architect, not waiting for AI to finish.

**4. 37% of companies fail at "tool awareness"**
They screen for "knows that Claude exists" instead of "can validate Claude's output." Knowing the tool ≠ applying it. The same trap catches candidates — naming RAG and agentic workflows fluently, but unable to debug the code behind them.

**5. Skeleton first, AI second**
Generate classes, interfaces, and function stubs first. Define the contract. Then ask AI to fill in logic. This prevents AI from steering your architecture — you keep the structural decisions.

The official guides tell you WHAT companies test. They don't tell you HOW it actually feels in the room.
