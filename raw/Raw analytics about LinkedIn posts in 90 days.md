    [Completed ISTQB Certified Tester Advanced Level - Test Management 3.0  
      
    After years in Software Testing, I was curious whether ISTQB still makes sense today. Sharing a few quick thoughts while it’s still fresh  
      
    👌 What worked well:  
    - The program has been updated from 2011 to 2024 and the content feels much more relevant today  
    - Plenty of international certification centres available  
    - The online (at-home) exam is similar to the in-person exam, but can be scheduled for any business hour and quickly (within 5 days)  
    - The syllabus gives a solid refresher on Testing within the SDLC  
    - Packed with information, but great for a quick refresh  
    - Examples of questions/answers really help to understand the exam logic and structure  
    - Trial exam from partners are helpful to train your timing skill  
    - Additional side-materials and apps(!) add real tremendous value for preparation  
      
    📛 What could be better:  
    - The language is sometimes overly bureaucratic or academic  
    - A few questions seem to require knowledge beyond the syllabus (e.g., TMMi, TPI), which feels useless for masses outside of corporates  
      
    🎤 Summary:  
    - For me, this certification is less about “a badge for the CV” and more about refreshing terminology and revisiting how we think about processes and metrics  
    - It’s also realistic to pass it through self-study - no formal training required for experienced Test Manager/QA Lead; just to be motivated for dozens of hours refreshing syllabus’ vocabulary and practicing timing using a simulator or examples  
      
    🦝 Curious, do you find certifications like ISTQB actually useful in practice or if they’re more of a structured refresher?

    [Some team scalability insights from my last startup  
      
    During 2023-2024, our testing team grew from 2 to 10 people. We faced two key hiring challenges: low candidate quality and low candidate volume, especially given our niche focus on Wi-Fi 🛜 and Linux.  
      
    1. Rethinking the candidate profile  
    We realized we were looking for the wrong profile. Instead of a typical QA engineer, we shifted toward network engineers willing to grow into QA.  
      
    This required building a structured onboarding and training program, along with mentorship from senior team members. The creation of such an educational ladder 🪜 is happening quite quickly and is available not only to corporations.  
    As a result, we were able to hire a strong Wi-Fi specialist every 2–3 months who quickly became productive.  
      
    2. Adjusting hiring strategy  
    After reviewing 100+ CVs and conducting 10+ interviews, I moved from a “hire slow, fire fast” approach to “hire fast, validate fast.”  
      
    We introduced 3-month probation periods focused on training, mentorship, and performance evaluation, with feedback from the whole team.  
    Only one candidate didn’t pass probation due to low contribution and communication gaps.  
      
    These changes significantly improved both hiring speed and team effectiveness. 📈
